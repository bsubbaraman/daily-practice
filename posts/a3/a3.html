ideation & setup

So many satisfying linkages in the world! I spent a while looking at Daniel Piker's (creator of Kangaroo) creations (link), as well as Twitter user Keishiro Ueki's linkages (link), and this repository of paper mechanisms from Kelli Anderson & Robby Kraft (link). Interested in these paper mechanisms and FoldMecha's craft linkages, I decided to use cardboard as my mechanism material. And while I was inspired by the many varieties of linkages in the world, I begin with a humble scissor linkage, since its motion is satisfyingly expressive to me.

I found and printed these cardboard rivets on Thingiverse to test. I found that a slightly scaled down size worked well.

scissor mechanism

Before making a physical mechanism, I simulated one in Grasshopper using Kangaroo to test varying links of varying lengths. I settled on links 100mm long and 25mm wide; the simulation would be particularly helpful if I wanted to combine mechanisms together to produce more complicated motion.

sensing state

Aligned with FoldMecha's findings about crafts extensibility, my original idea to sense state was to use conductive fabric in a voltage divider, and calibrate it such that stretching the fabric would move anchor points in Rhino. The values weren't nearly consistent enough to do this though, at least with my very lo-fi prototype.


conductive fabric taped to handle


Then I played around with mounting one linkage to a servo to be able to set its position, but the little servo I had at home wasn't powerful enough. In the end, I returned to the conductive fabric to make a button to sense a closed state. When closed, the Kangaroo Solver component's reset input is triggered, closing the virtual mechanism.

reflections

I'm happy to get some exposure to Kangaroo to use in future projects. In particular, it'd be fun to send data to microcontroller to control a project based on a Grasshopper simulation; I'm imagining silly little linkage plotters. The appeal to 'craft' through conductive fabric is a bit half-hearted here; I think that combining the expressivity of mechanism motion with e.g. the creative spirit of Kobakant would make for some engaging artifacts!
